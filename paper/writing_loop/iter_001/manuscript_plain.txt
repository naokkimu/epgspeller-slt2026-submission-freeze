=== 01_introduction.tex ===
 For users and usage scenarios that cannot rely on speech or hand movement, hands-free character entry pathways are important. Silent speech interfaces are a promising direction for this requirement, and research has progressed on inferring linguistic intent from articulatory movements without vocalization, biosignals, and intraoral signals~. However, character entry for augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) requires more than recognizing a small set of predefined commands. Users need to compose names, medication labels, idiosyncratic expressions, and short messages on the spot. Accordingly, bringing silent input closer to AAC requires spelling recognition---decoding character strings---rather than recognition confined to a closed vocabulary. Existing silent speech interfaces have already demonstrated important capabilities. Studies report recognizing silent or low-volume speech intent using biosignals, perioral motion, tongue and lip imagery, in-ear sensing, and near-whisper input~. Recent work also explores cross-modal learning and large language models for assisted decoding, expanding the output layer of silent speech recognition from simple classifiers toward language-model-supported character entry. These results show that silent signals can serve as practical input interfaces. At the same time, achieving high accuracy under constrained recognition is not the same as enabling users to spell unseen or context-dependent messages on the spot. AAC-oriented character entry requires not only high accuracy within a fixed vocabulary, but also character-level decoding that extends beyond that vocabulary. Electropalatography (EPG) offers a concrete input signal for this problem. EPG records tongue--palate contact during articulation as multichannel temporal patterns and has been used in speech production research, speech training, and articulatory analysis~. The SmartPalate-style EPG considered in this paper is a removable intraoral sensor with multiple electrodes on the palate; at each time step, a frame represents a contact pattern indicating where on the palate contact occurs. Each trial is treated as a pair consisting of a variable-length EPG contact time series and the word label that the participant attempted to spell. Although this signal is not acoustic speech itself, it directly encodes temporal changes in tongue--palate contact during silent articulation and therefore occupies a natural position as input for silent spelling recognition. The feasibility of this input modality was already demonstrated by prior work on SilentSpeller. SilentSpeller showed that SmartPalate-style EPG silent spelling can function as a portable, hands-free character entry modality~. This paper addresses the recognition-layer problem that supports that input modality. SilentSpeller established the premise that EPG silent spelling can work as an input modality; on that premise, we directly address the remaining recognition problem---lexicon-free string decoding and the calibration bottleneck it entails. Silent command recognition, language-model-assisted character entry, and in-ear silent spelling with ReHEarSSE~ are all related, but the question in this paper is how far lexicon-free spelling sequences can be decoded from EPG contact sequences. Fixed-vocabulary constraints are especially important for AAC-oriented silent input. In fixed-vocabulary recognition, the words a system can output depend on a candidate set defined at training or evaluation time. In this setting, recognition errors can be reduced through dictionary constraints or language-model assistance, but the range of spellable words is simultaneously restricted. Character-level silent spelling shifts the input problem from selecting a candidate word to composing a character string. If spelling sequences are decoded accurately enough, users are not limited to words that appeared as output classes during training. In this respect, character-level silent spelling recognition is an important pathway from fixed-command recognition toward an AAC-oriented character entry layer. We present EPGSpeller, a recognizer that decodes character strings from EPG contact time series, to address this recognition problem. EPGSpeller is a sequence recognizer that maps contact frame sequences directly to character strings. To align variable-length contact sequences with string labels of differing length, we use connectionist temporal classification (CTC), which can learn variable-length inputs and string labels without frame-level character boundaries~. The primary evaluation is based on character-level decoding without vocabulary lists, language models, or lexicon projection. Results obtained with dictionary constraints are reported as supplementary diagnostics under closed-vocabulary conditions. This distinguishes the paper from fixed-vocabulary command recognition and recognition with lexicon projection, and evaluates character-level EPG silent spelling recognition on its own terms. The central claim of this paper is that, with per-user calibration, EPG silent spelling can support a recognition layer for lexicon-free string decoding that is not bound to a fixed command vocabulary. At the same time, we foreground its limits. When the same recognizer is transferred directly to a new user, performance degrades substantially. This indicates that cross-user differences in contact patterns, articulatory style, and sensor placement strongly govern recognition performance. Accordingly, the central remaining challenge at present is neither the feasibility of the input modality nor inference speed, but how much calibration data is sufficient per new user---calibration-efficient personalized recognition. To support this claim, the experiments in this paper are organized around four questions. First, under within-user conditions, how accurately can lexicon-free character strings be decoded from EPG contact sequences? Second, connecting to prior SilentSpeller evaluation, how much can a modern string decoder strengthen the recognition layer within the same input modality? Third, we evaluate calibration with a small amount of target-user data and quantify cross-user transfer degradation as a calibration-data problem. Fourth, we examine multi-source transfer and simple preprocessing controls to confirm that user dependence is not merely a normalization issue. This paper makes three contributions. First, we formulate SmartPalate-style EPG silent spelling as a lexicon-free string decoding problem rather than fixed-command recognition, and present an evaluation protocol suite (P1, P2, P3, and $k$-shot) that compares within-user, unseen-word, cross-user transfer, and few-shot calibration conditions within a single task. Second, under this framework, we conduct a calibration feasibility study and quantify both the viability of the recognition layer and the calibration bottleneck from within-user conditions (P1: CER {0.0844}; P2: CER {0.0627}) and few-shot calibration ($k=1$: CER {0.0600}). Third, we show that cross-user transfer (P3: CER {0.1332}) does not reach within-user performance even with multi-source transfer or preprocessing controls, and report that user dependence remains an essential calibration problem. The novelty of this paper lies not in the CTC recognizer architecture, but in this task and protocol formulation together with the calibration feasibility study. 

=== 02_signal_task.tex ===
 The recognizer receives not an acoustic waveform or video, but a multichannel time series representing tongue--palate contact, and outputs not a closed set of word classes but a character string. Each trial is treated as a variable-length sequence of EPG contact frames, and the input received by EPGSpeller is this frame sequence. === 03_system.tex ===
 This section defines the EPGSpeller recognition system. EPGSpeller provides a recognition layer for lexicon-free character-string decoding on top of the input modality demonstrated by SilentSpeller~. EPGSpeller is a sequence recognizer that takes electropalatographic contact frame sequences and outputs the corresponding character strings. Each trial is a variable-length contact sequence; character boundaries and phoneme boundaries are not provided. The primary output path is lexicon-free greedy character-string decoding defined in Section~. In silent-spelling EPG sequences, the correspondence between each frame and a character is not explicit. Participants articulate continuously, and tongue--palate contact is not clearly segmented at the character level. EPGSpeller uses connectionist temporal classification (CTC) for this alignment~. CTC learns sequence outputs without frame-level labels by collapsing frame-level outputs that include blank symbols and repetitions into the final character string. In the main recognizer, each electropalatographic frame is treated as a flat contact vector. The temporal recognizer is a GRU-based recurrent CTC decoder. We treat this vector/GRU CTC path as the main recognizer throughout the paper. The central claim that electropalatographic silent spelling can support lexicon-free character-string decoding when personalized calibration is available is evaluated on this main recognizer and greedy decoding. Multichannel contact frames are mapped through an input projection into a representation that the sequence recognizer can process efficiently. Along the time axis, compression reduces sequence length while preserving local context. During training, we apply dropout, noise injection, smoothing, and SpecAugment-style temporal and channel regularization to improve robustness to contact-position variability, missing channels, and participant-specific differences~. SmartPalate contact channels have a layout on the palate. We compare row-column, grid, and patch front ends to test whether spatial structure helps electropalatographic silent-spelling recognition. The row-column front end summarizes contact patterns on the surrogate palatal grid into row-wise and column-wise activity. Grid and patch front ends use local spatial structure more explicitly, but electropalatographic contact is sparse and highly participant-dependent, so a strong two-dimensional spatial assumption is not always advantageous. Results in Section~ show that the flat vector/GRU path remains competitive on P1. We also compare contact-frequency-based selection, spatially dispersed selection, ranking transfer from other participants, and random subsets to examine whether recognition performance can be preserved with fewer channels. Electrode reduction and missing-channel robustness are engineering analyses; they are not primary means of solving cross-user transfer. Later analyses indicate that $K=64$ channels retain near-main P1/P2 performance for the selection methods tested. The main recognizer connects a CTC output layer to a bidirectional GRU ($n_ =1024$, $n_ =5$); input projection maps contact-channel representations to 256 dimensions. Along time, compression with kernel length~32 and stride~4 reduces sequence length. The output has 40 classes (26 alphabet letters, blank, CTC blank, and other special symbols). Training uses batch size~64, fixed learning rate~0.02, L2 regularization coefficient $10^{-5}$, dropout rate~0.4, and 10{,}000 training steps. Data augmentation applies SpecAugment-style temporal masking ($T=12$, rate~0.4), electrode masking ($F=6$, rate~0.4), and time warping ($W=0.05$, rate~0.2), plus noise and smoothing. Results are averaged over four trials with random seeds 0--3; mean and standard deviation are reported. In PCA variants, 120 contact channels are compressed to 16 principal components before input projection. Key hyperparameters are recorded in . === 04_evaluation.tex ===
 This section fixes how EPGSpeller is compared across evaluation conditions and which metrics support the central claims. The core evaluation of EPGSpeller is character-string decoding without lexicon projection. Within-user recognition, unseen-word recognition, cross-user transfer, and low-shot calibration each answer a distinct question. EPGSpeller evaluation treats the questions needed for AAC-oriented silent spelling separately. Within-user conditions measure how accurately character strings can be decoded when the recognizer is trained on data from the target user. Word-holdout conditions measure whether spelling recognition generalizes to word labels not seen during training. Cross-user transfer conditions measure whether a recognizer trained on other users can be reused without target-user calibration data. Low-shot calibration conditions measure how much performance recovers when a few calibration examples are obtained from a new user. P1 (word holdout, within user) is the primary evidence for lexicon-free spelling with personalized calibration. P2 (instance holdout, within user) is treated as a near-upper-bound within-user condition. P3 (cross-user transfer) is the primary evidence for the calibration bottleneck. Multi-source transfer, low-shot calibration, and the SilentSpeller bridge condition are auxiliary. Direct comparison with the SilentSpeller-era recognizer and modern alternative decoders (beam search, language-model integration, attention-based encoder--decoder, and others) is outside the scope of this paper and is left for future work. === 06_discussion.tex ===
 The main result is that SmartPalate-style EPG silent spelling can support lexicon-free string decoding when user calibration is available. Under P1 word holdout and P2 instance holdout, greedy string decoding before lexicon projection yields low CER; under P3 cross-user transfer, error increases substantially. In this framing, lexicon-free decoding is the enabling recognition-layer result, but calibration quantity---not decoder architecture---explains most of the gap between P1/P2 and P3. Relative to the input modality established by SilentSpeller, this paper clarifies how far the recognition layer can be strengthened and where the calibration problem remains. SilentSpeller showed that SmartPalate-style EPG silent spelling can function as a hands-free character-entry modality~; this paper addresses the recognition layer that supports that modality. Our question is not whether the modality works as an input method, but how accurately lexicon-free strings can be decoded and with how little calibration. The most important limitation in this work is calibration, not inference speed. Cross-user transfer degrades sharply, and multi-source transfer plus simple preprocessing controls do not close the gap to within-user performance. Low-shot results show that zero-shot transfer is weak but a few calibration examples from the target user yield large recovery; high-$k$ calibration curves remain constrained by per-word repetition density in the public data. The ability to decode strings hands-free without vocalization is potentially useful in settings where speech or hand input cannot be assumed. We provide offline recognition-layer evidence that lexicon-free string decoding can work with user calibration; fit, hygiene, correction interfaces, candidate presentation, and user learning burden remain system-design questions beyond this scope, and deployment studies must eventually measure correction burden and fatigue in addition to offline CER. This paper is a protocol and calibration-feasibility study on a public EPG spelling release, not a deployed AAC product evaluation. EPG records intraoral contact patterns from the SilentSpeller public release~ under that paper's consent conditions; EPGSpeller performs offline evaluation only, with dedicated devices per participant. Limitations include four healthy participants (not clinical AAC users), offline recognition without interactive speed or comfort evaluation, a SilentSpeller bridge that differs from that system's interactive setup, infeasible high-$k$ held-word protocols at current repetition density ($k=4$ on 14 words for p3), and deferred external decoder baselines (in-task architectural baselines in Table~ bound representation choices instead). Reproducibility: train/test/competition partitions, per-split sample counts, and label multiplicities are logged per protocol and seed; hyperparameters and CSV exports are recorded in the public repository for independent verification. 

=== 07_conclusion.tex ===
 This paper makes three contributions aligned with the introduction. First, we formulate SmartPalate-style EPG silent spelling as a lexicon-free string-decoding problem and present an evaluation protocol family---P1, P2, P3, and $k$-shot---that compares within-user recognition, unseen words, cross-user transfer, and low-shot calibration within one task. Second, under user-calibrated conditions (P1/P2) and with low-shot calibration ($k=1$), we quantify the feasibility of the recognition layer (Table~). Third, we show that cross-user transfer (P3) does not reach within-user performance even with multi-source transfer, preprocessing controls, or in-task architectural baselines (Table~), and report that calibration efficiency is the next central challenge. We do not claim user-independent deployment or superiority over external decoder families deferred to future work. The next central challenge is efficient personalized recognition with minimal calibration; future work requires short calibration sessions, source-user selection, LM-assisted correction, and denser per-word repetition datasets, while deployment evaluation must move beyond offline CER to correction burden, fatigue, and wearability. Replication is supported by exported splits, feasibility counts, and table-level CSVs in the public results tree.
