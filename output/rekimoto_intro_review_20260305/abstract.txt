
Silent spelling with electropalatography is a promising route to hands-free text entry, but many evaluations still mix word-identity generalization, instance-level memorization, and participant transfer, which blurs what actually improves. We present EPGSpeller, an evidence-only evaluation framework for character-level silent spelling from binary palate-contact sequences. EPGSpeller fixes deterministic protocols for word holdout, instance holdout, and cross-participant transfer, and it pins split and metric artifacts so each reported result is reproducible. Under this shared harness, we compare a vector baseline with row-column and grid-based spatial front ends, and we also evaluate electrode reduction and low-shot adaptation. Across audited runs, row-column pooling tracks the vector baseline more consistently than grid-based variants, while cross-participant transfer remains the dominant bottleneck. These results show that protocol auditability is a first-order requirement for defensible progress in EPG silent spelling.
